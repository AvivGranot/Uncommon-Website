Essay 05

"Invest in preparedness, not in prediction"

Nassim Nicholas Taleb

The paradox, as illustrated in the book The Black Swan by the great Nassim Taleb, is that we cannot predict Black Swans. Nassim attaches three attributes to Black Swans: rarity, consequences, and retrospective explainability. Yet, Uncommon People are themselves a higher degree of Black Swans. They're not just mere events as Nassim describes. Uncommonness is the life of a single person, affecting generations to come, at scale. Which leaves us to question: if Black Swans are unpredictable, how can we predict Uncommonness? My intellect can only be inferior to Nassim's; therefore, I should stick with his arguments that we simply cannot. There are too many known and unknown variables, and too much luck. In this essay, I'll cover and provide examples from the life of George Washington. Washington illustrates better than any theory how the fate of one man cannot be predicted, and how a chain of unexpected, even tragic, events can lead to Uncommonness.

At the age of eleven, Washington lost his father, Augustine. Augustine bequeathed land to George and to his half-siblings. As a consequence, George received a seat at the table of Virginia's gentry. By seventeen, he was appointed the official surveyor of Culpeper County, a position of influence and prestige far beyond the reach of most young men of his time. In his early twenties, tragedy struck again when his half-brother Lawrence died. Lawrence had played a key role in influencing George's upbringing. Over the years, several other siblings perished from the earth as well. Washington inherited their share of the family estate, including the now-famous Mount Vernon plantation. I shall not summarize the work of the admired scholar Ron Chernow and his 904-page biography of Washington here (self-recommending).

Almost immediately thereafter, in 1752, Governor Robert Dinwiddie appointed George to the role of adjutant of Virginia's southern district, his first formal military role, earning £100 a year. Paradoxically, the foundation of Washington's meteoric rise in society, wealth, and responsibility was also due to his grief and misfortunes. How could anyone, including George, predict the loss of a father, of several siblings, and of his half-brother?

Even on the battlefield, Washington's presence seemed touched by fate: he would ride openly on a white horse, erected such that contemporaries shared stories of how they were inspired by and in awe of his fearless posture. Musket fire grazed his coat numerous times on different battlefields, two horses were shot from under him, yet Washington emerged unscathed. Since I fought on battlefields too, I can support, from my own experience, that even the best soldiers must carry with them a lot of luck.

Nothing in Washington's circumstances could have foretold the life of an uncommon person today. How can we derive lessons then? The answer is partly what Taleb claims: we can study the part that converts the Black Swan from black to gray: Washington's personality, judgment, values, and beliefs. Whether right or wrong, even in his mistakes, there are lessons to be derived. A minor yet profound example is Washington's decision to willingly relinquish power after serving two terms as president (the only exception was FDR, who served as president for four terms until his death in 1945). Shall you run a very quick Google and view the countries with the longest-presiding presidents. Small, yet significant, Washington paved the path to the foremost democratic nation in the history of the world. We can enumerate the countless choices Washington made in leading the Continental Army to become victorious over the British Empire, and to co-found the United States of America. Setting the path to future enlightenment, Washington said the following about slavery: "I rejoice in a belief that intellectual light will spring up in the dark corners of the earth; that freedom of inquiry will produce liberality of conduct; that mankind will reverse the absurd position that the many were made for the few; and that they will not continue slaves in one part of the globe, when they can become freemen in another." (page 556, Washington: A Life, by Ron Chernow).

To conclude: Go in depth; learn about and from uncommon people. It is widely known that one should not learn how to sell from someone who never sold. Or, that you don't want to learn math from someone who's wrong in the fundamentals, teaching you incorrect rules and logic. The time to fix the latter is greater than the time to study correctly. Therefore, as I said: go in depth, learn from those whose route you wish to follow. And shall you convert the black swan to gray.
